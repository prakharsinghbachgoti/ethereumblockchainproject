The first stage is exploratory analysis on each dataset separately, before you connect anything. For the fee data, you'd pull historical gas prices and group them by hour of day and day of week to see the actual congestion pattern, then check whether that pattern is stable over time or drifting (since Ethereum's fee market has changed structurally a few times, like after EIP-1559 and after some scaling upgrades), because if the pattern shifted recently your forecast model needs to be trained mostly on recent data, not years of history. For the whale data, you'd first need to decide what counts as a "whale move," meaning picking a dollar threshold and also pulling a list of known exchange wallet addresses, then count how many qualifying transfers happen per day or week just to get a feel for how frequent this signal even is, since if it only happens once a month it's not a very useful live signal.
The second stage is the actual hypothesis test on the whale side, which is the most important analytical piece of the whole project. For every historical whale-to-exchange transfer you find, you'd look at what the price did in the hours and days afterward, and compare that to what the price does on average during random time windows. That comparison is the core finding: does the price actually move down more often after these events than it would by chance, and if so by how much. You'd also check for false positives, like transfers that turned out to be internal exchange housekeeping rather than a deposit ahead of a sale, since that affects how trustworthy the signal really is.
The third stage is building and validating the fee forecast itself as an analysis task, not a coding task. That means choosing how far ahead you're forecasting (the next hour vs the next 24 hours), splitting your historical data into a training period and a holdout period, generating predictions for the holdout period, and then measuring how far off those predictions actually were from the real fees that happened. This is where you produce the number you'll want to quote later, like "predictions were within X% of actual fees on average."
The fourth stage is combining the two signals and analyzing what happens when they co-occur, which is the part that makes this more than two separate side projects. You'd look at historical periods where a whale deposit and a fee spike happened close together in time, and see whether that combination preceded bigger or more reliable price moves than the whale signal alone. This is genuinely original analysis since it's not something you'll find a pre-built answer for anywhere, you'd be generating it yourself from the data.
The last stage is writing up the findings honestly, including the parts that didn't work as well as you hoped, since a believable, well-reasoned negative result on the whale signal is more valuable for your portfolio than an overstated positive one.
